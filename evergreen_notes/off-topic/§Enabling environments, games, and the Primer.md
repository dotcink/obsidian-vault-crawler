# §Enabling environments, games, and the Primer

**(This turned into a full essay [here](https://andymatuschak.org/primer). These notes are now pretty outdated.)**

Rather than focusing on the Primer, I’m finding a more powerful framing to be in centering the essay around scaling high-growth environments.

- You can read a book about starting a startup, or you can join [[Y Combinator]]. Which do you choose? This is the power of an [[Enabling environment]].
	- The latter’s a much more effective growth environment, and it’s mostly not the funding.
		- When you read the book, you’re a founder wanna-be reading about the activities of founders, and you’re not “practicing founding”; when you do YC, you’re *doing founding,* in a community of founders. The book’s abstract, but YC’s not: every lesson is in the context of the idea you care deeply about:
		- [[Enabling environments’ activities directly serve an intrinsically meaningful purpose]]
		- [[Enabling environments focus on doing what’s enabled]]
		- [[Enabling environments focus on creating opportunities for growth and action, not on skill-building]]
		- The difference isn’t just emotional: you’re going to learn this material much more effectively in the context of YC because it’s a [[Participatory environment]]: [[Participatory environments support learning]]
		- The format also allows its designers to author founders’ experience over a multi-month period, while [[Mass mediums mostly lack an authored time dimension beyond a day]]
		- The authentic purpose doesn’t just improve the experience for participating founders: it also helps YC’s creators design much better systems.
		- [[Powerful enabling environments usually arise as a byproduct of projects pursuing their own intrinsically meaningful purposes]]
		- [[Effective system design requires insights drawn from serious contexts of use]]
		- Of course, YC’s lessons aren’t “lessons” in a traditional sense—they’re experienced as the natural consequence of your actions in that environment: YC is an [[Enacted experience]].
		- Even though you don’t know what you’re doing as a founder ([[Novices in enabling environments often can’t do what’s enabled]]), YC’s systems give you enough support to participate authentically and hone your skills in this intense context: [[Enacted experiences can bootstrap active participation in enabling environments]]
		- But those experiences aren’t an accident: YC’s enacted lessons are carefully *authored*, in the same sense that a book is. [[Enacted experiences can create intense personal connection to authored targets]]; [[Enacted experiences amplify the power of narrative]].
- Unfortunately, [[Enacted experiences are hard to author]] and [[Enacted experiences are hard to distribute]].
	- But there’s hope: [[Enacted experiences have incredible potential as a mass medium]]
- Video game design is the best prior art we have for this.
	- (some core notes on games and enacted experiences in [[Enacted experience]]; needs to be refactored)
		- emotional experiences and narrative unfold as a consequence of player action
		- and they’re good at building skills: [[Games effectively develop players’ skills]]
		- games demonstrate how dynamic media can do things that books can’t do: [[Games help players evaluate their developing skills]] and [[Games help players make and adapt plans]]
		- but games are an aesthetic medium; they’re about [[“Find the fun”]]; they’re not externally-oriented like non-fiction and YC: [[Skill development in games is subservient to other intrinsically meaningful purposes]]
- this is the core mistake that educational games make
	- [[Educational games try to teach through enacted experiences]]
		- [[Educational games are a doomed approach to creating enabling environments]]
		- (lots more notes here from that subtree and the anti-educationalism tag; [[Educational objectives often subvert themselves]] is a good root ==todo structure subtree in outline==)
- introducing [[The Young Lady’s Illustrated Primer]]:
	- [[The Primer reminds people of their own experiences with profound enablement]]
		- The Primer seems like a much better example than educational games: [[The Primer’s goal is to produce creative, subversive youth]]; it’s not about checking a bunch of learning objective boxes
		- Like games, [[The Primer is an enormous enacted experience]], and [[The Primer is scalable]]
		- Unfortunately, the specific mechanics described in *The Diamond Age* wouldn’t work: see [[The Primer isn’t a viable enabling environment]] and subtree ==todo structure subtree in outline==
		- it’s hard to imagine modifying educational games to become anything good, but the Primer is closer enough to the mark to be worth criticizing, and, possibly, to learn from
		- Nell does actually feel like she’s bringing each step about; that’s not true in edu games because [[Enacted experiences require participant-situated cause and effect]]
		- the Primer is about things that really matter ([[The Primer’s goal is to produce creative, subversive youth]], even if Nell doesn’t know it ([[Nell doesn’t know or share the Primer’s goals]])
		- activities in the Primer are much closer to an authentic enabling environment [[Enacted experiences can bootstrap active participation in enabling environments]]
		- it could be made continuous with expert environments, rather than something to leave behind ([[The Primer is one big “onboarding” experience for the rest of Nell’s life]])
